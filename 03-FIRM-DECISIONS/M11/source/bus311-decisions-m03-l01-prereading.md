# Capital Structure and Financing Decisions

## Why this matters

WACC summarizes financing costs; capital structure determines the claims and constraints behind them. This lesson uses **Delta Air Lines** as a decision context. The goal is not merely to reproduce a formula; it is to explain what the result means for a financial decision and what evidence could change the conclusion.

## Learning objectives

- Compare debt and equity trade-offs
- Evaluate financing capacity and flexibility
- Write a defensible CFO recommendation

## Debt and equity trade-offs

### Debt advantages

Contractual payments and maturity discipline can reduce agency costs. The benefit is strongest when taxable income is stable and default risk remains manageable.

### Debt costs

Distress costs include more than bankruptcy fees. Customers, employees, suppliers, and managers may change behavior long before a formal filing.

### Equity flexibility

Common equity has no required payment or maturity, making it resilient. Issuance can be expensive when information asymmetry or undervaluation is high.

## What determines capacity?

### Cash-flow stability

Cyclicality, operating leverage, customer concentration, and commodity exposure reduce safe debt capacity even when current ratios look strong.

### Asset support

Tangible, marketable assets can support secured borrowing. Specialized or intangible assets often provide weaker recovery value in distress.

## Build the recommendation

### Credit consequences

A financing plan should test leverage, coverage, liquidity, maturity concentration, and covenant capacity under downside conditions.

### Financial flexibility

The cheapest financing today may be costly if it prevents the company from acting later. Flexibility has option value even when it is hard to observe directly.

### CFO memo

A decision-ready recommendation quantifies expected value, identifies the main downside, explains alternatives, and names indicators that would cause management to revisit the plan.

## Worked example

**Excel:** `=EBIT/interest_expense`

**Manual logic:** $1.2 billion EBIT ÷ $300 million interest

**Expected conclusion:** 4.0× base coverage; rerun under the downside EBIT case.

The workbook compares project financing choices and requires a recommendation grounded in cash flow, coverage, and flexibility.

## Decision questions

1. Why might Delta choose equity even when debt appears cheaper?
1. Which downside assumption matters most for debt capacity?

## Key takeaways

- Debt adds both tax benefit and fixed risk.
- Capacity depends on downside cash flow.
- Flexibility belongs in the recommendation.
